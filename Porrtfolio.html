Black-Scholes formula derivation

Under the Black-Scholes framework, the price of a stock follows a geometric Brownian motion given by the stochastic differential equation: $$ dS_t = \mu S_t dt + S_t \sigma^\top dW_t $$

where:

  • $S_t$ is the stock price at time $t$
  • $\mu$ is the drift rate of the stock price
  • $\sigma$ is the constant volatility vector $d$-dimensional of the stock price
  • $W_t$ is a standard Brownian motion vector $d$-dimensional

Under the risk-neutral measure, the drift $\mu$ is replaced by the risk-free rate $r$, leading to the risk-neutral dynamics: $$ dS_t = r S_t dt + S_t \sigma^\top dW_t^Q $$ where $W_t^Q$ is a $d$-dimensional independent Brownian motions under the risk-neutral measure $Q$.

Hence, solving the above SDE as follows: $$ \begin{aligned} d(\ln S_t) &= \frac{1}{S_t} dS_t - \frac{{(dS_t)}^2}{2S_t^2} \\ &=\frac{1}{S_t}(r S_t dt + S_t \sigma^\top dW_t^Q) - \frac{1}{2S_t^2} (S_t^2\sigma^\top\sigma dt)\\ &= \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) dt + \sigma^\top dW_t^Q\\ \Rightarrow \int_0^T d(\ln S_t) &= \int_0^T \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) dt + \int_0^T \sigma^\top dW_t^Q\\ \Rightarrow \ln S_T - \ln S_0 &= \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T + \sigma^\top W_T^Q\\ \Rightarrow \ln S_T &\overset{Q}{\sim} \mathcal{N}\left(\ln S_0 + \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T, \sigma^\top\sigma T\right)\\ \end{aligned} $$

The price of a European call option $C$ with strike price $K$ and maturity $T$ is given by the discounted expected payoff under the risk-neutral measure:

$$ \begin{aligned} C &= e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^Q[(S_T - K)^+]\\ &= e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^Q[(S_T - K)\mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}]\\ &= e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^Q\left[S_T \mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] - e^{-rT} K \mathbb{E}^Q\left[\mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right]\\ \end{aligned} $$ where $(x)^+ = \max(x, 0)$.

There are many ways to solve this expectation. The two most common methods are:

  • Perform the integration directly using the log-normal distribution of $S_T$.
  • Use the change of measure technique (Girsanov's theorem) to simplify the expectation.

1. Direct integration method:

With this method, we can compute the two expectations separately. The second expectation is easier to compute: $$ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}^Q\left[\mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] &= \mathbb{P}^Q(S_T > K)\\ &= \mathbb{P}^Q\left(\ln S_T > \ln K\right)\\ &= \Phi\left(\frac{\ln S_0 + \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T - \ln K}{\sqrt{\sigma^\top\sigma T}}\right)\\ &= \Phi(d_2)\\ \end{aligned} $$ where $\Phi(\cdot)$ is the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the standard normal distribution and $d_2 = \frac{\ln\frac{S_0}{K} + \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T}{\sqrt{\sigma^\top\sigma T}}$.

In order to solve the first expectation, we need to have a lemma that helps to compute the truncated expectation of a log-normal distribution faster, which is known as the Gaussian Shift Lemma:

Lemma (Gaussian Shift Lemma): Given $x$ as follow: $$ \begin{align} x &\sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\\ \phi(x) &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma^2}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \end{align} $$ where $\phi(x)$ is the probability density function (PDF) of $x$. Then, for any constant $a,b,c \in \mathbb{R}$, we have: $$ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[e^{ax+b} \mathbb{1}_{\{x > c\}}] &= \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{ax+b} \mathbb{1}_{\{x > c\}} \phi(x) dx\\ &= \int_{c}^\infty e^{ax+b} \phi(x) dx\\ &= e^b\int_{c}^\infty e^{ax} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma^2}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} dx\\ &= e^b\int_{c}^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma^2}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2-2\sigma^2ax}{2\sigma^2}} dx\\ &= e^b\int_{c}^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma^2}}e^{-\frac{(x-(\mu+\sigma^2a))^2 - (\mu+\sigma^2a)^2 + \mu^2}{2\sigma^2}} dx\\ &= e^{\mu a + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 a^2 + b} \int_{c}^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi \sigma^2}}e^{-\frac{(x-(\mu+\sigma^2a))^2}{2\sigma^2}} dx\\ &= e^{\mu a + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 a^2 + b} \left(1 - \Phi\left(\frac{c - (\mu + \sigma^2 a)}{\sigma}\right)\right)\\ &= e^{\mu a + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 a^2 + b} \Phi\left(\frac{\mu + \sigma^2 a - c}{\sigma}\right)\\ \end{aligned} $$

Applying the lemma with $x = \ln S_T$, $a = 1$, $b = 0$, $c = \ln K$, $\mu = \ln S_0 + \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T$, and $\sigma^2 = \sigma^\top\sigma T$, we have: $$ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}^Q\left[S_T \mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] &= \mathbb{E}^Q\left[e^{\ln S_T} \mathbb{1}_{\{\ln S_T > \ln K\}}\right]\\ &= e^{\mu + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2} \Phi\left(\frac{\mu + \sigma^2 - c}{\sigma}\right)\\ &= e^{\ln S_0 + \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma T} \Phi\left(\frac{\ln S_0 + \left(r - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T + \sigma^\top\sigma T - \ln K}{\sqrt{\sigma^\top\sigma T}}\right)\\ &= S_0 e^{rT} \Phi(d_1)\\ \end{aligned} $$ where $d_1 = \frac{\ln\frac{S_0}{K} + \left(r + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T}{\sqrt{\sigma^\top\sigma T}}$.

Combining the two expectations, we have the Black-Scholes formula for European call option price: $$ \begin{aligned} C &= e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^Q\left[S_T \mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] - e^{-rT} K \mathbb{E}^Q\left[\mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right]\\ &= e^{-rT} \left(S_0 e^{rT} \Phi(d_1)\right) - e^{-rT} K \Phi(d_2)\\ &= S_0 \Phi(d_1) - K e^{-rT} \Phi(d_2)\\ \end{aligned} $$

2. Change of measure method:

Under the risk-neutral measure, we have: $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{S_T}{e^{rT}} \frac{1}{S_0}&= \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma T + \sigma^\top W_T^Q\right)\\ \end{aligned} $$ is a valid exponential martingale. Hence, we can use the above ratio to define a new probability measure $\tilde{Q}$ with the Radon-Nikodym derivative as follows: $$ \frac{d\tilde{Q}}{dQ} = \frac{S_T}{e^{rT}} \frac{1}{S_0} $$ The Girsanov's theorem states that under the new measure $\tilde{Q}$, the process: $$ \tilde{W}_t = W_t^Q - \sigma t $$ is a standard Brownian motion. Therefore, we can rewrite the stock price dynamics under the new measure $\tilde{Q}$ as follows: $$ \begin{aligned} dS_t &= r S_t dt + S_t \sigma^\top dW_t^Q \\ &= r S_t dt + S_t \sigma^\top (d\tilde{W}_t + \sigma dt)\\ &= (r + \sigma^\top\sigma) S_t dt + S_t \sigma^\top d\tilde{W}_t\\ \end{aligned} $$ Solving the above SDE, we have: $$ \begin{aligned} \ln S_T &\overset{\tilde{Q}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}\left(\ln S_0 + \left(r + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T, \sigma^\top\sigma T\right)\\ \end{aligned} $$ Using the change of measure, we can rewrite the first expectation as follows: $$ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}^Q\left[S_T \mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] &= \mathbb{E}^{\tilde{Q}}\left[\frac{dQ}{d\tilde{Q}} S_T \mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right]\\ &= \mathbb{E}^{\tilde{Q}}\left[\frac{e^{rT} S_0}{S_T} S_T \mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right]\\ &= e^{rT} S_0 \mathbb{E}^{\tilde{Q}}\left[\mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right]\\ &= e^{rT} S_0 \mathbb{P}^{\tilde{Q}}(S_T > K)\\ &= e^{rT} S_0 \mathbb{P}^{\tilde{Q}}(\ln S_T > \ln K)\\ &= e^{rT} S_0 \Phi\left(\frac{\ln S_0 + \left(r + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^\top\sigma\right) T - \ln K}{\sqrt{\sigma^\top\sigma T}}\right)\\ &= e^{rT} S_0 \Phi(d_1)\\ \end{aligned} $$ Combining the two expectations, we have the Black-Scholes formula for European call option price: $$ \begin{aligned} C &= e^{-rT} \mathbb{E}^Q\left[S_T \mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] - e^{-rT} K \mathbb{E}^Q\left[\mathbb{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right]\\ &= e^{-rT} \left(e^{rT} S_0 \Phi(d_1)\right) - e^{-rT} K \Phi(d_2)\\ &= S_0 \Phi(d_1) - K e^{-rT} \Phi(d_2)\\ \end{aligned} $$

For the more complex structured option, the change of measure technique is often more convenient to use than the direct integration method. This is because the change of measure can simplify the expectations significantly by transforming them into probabilities under the new measure, which are often easier to compute. This will be illustrated in the a different project.

Black-Scholes formula implementation

In[2]:
def BlackScholes(S, K, sgm, r, T, callput):
    d1 = (np.log(S / K) + (r + 0.5 * sgm**2) * T) / (sgm * np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sgm * np.sqrt(T)

    if callput not in [1, -1]:
        raise ValueError("callput must be 1 for call or -1 for put")
    return callput * (
        S * norm.cdf(callput * d1) - K * np.exp(-r * T) * norm.cdf(callput * d2)
    )

Monte Carlo pricing of a Black/Scholes call option

Another way to price a European option is to use Monte Carlo simulation. Instead of solving the expectation analytically, we can approximate it using random sampling. In the Black/Scholes model, the price of the underlying stock follows Geometric Brownian motion, with the dynamics under the risk-neutral measure given by $$S(T)=S(0)\exp\left\{\left(r\frac12\sigma^\top\sigma\right)T+\sigma^\top W^Q(T)\right\}$$ where $W^Q(T)$ is a standard Brownian motion vector $d$-dimensional under the risk-neutral measure which has the distribution $W^Q(T) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, T I_d)$.

Recall that the time 0 price of a European call option (and analogously the put option) expiring at time $T$ with strike price $K$ can be expressed as the expectation under the risk-neutral measure of $$C=E\left[e^{rT}\max(0,S(T)K)\right]$$ Thus we can write a Python function which simulates the terminal stock prices using Monte Carlo sampling and computes the option price as the discounted average of the payoffs. The function also calculates the standard deviation of the simulation mean to provide an estimate of the pricing error.

Error estimation for Monte Carlo methods

Monte Carlo simulation without error bounds is meaningless. By the Central Limit Theorem, we know that for a large number $N$ of simulations, the simulation mean $\mu$ is approximately normally distributed, with standard deviation $\sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}N}$ where the simulation variance is an estimate for $\sigma^2$.

Thus, if there is no bias, the simulation mean is normally distributed around the target value with a standard deviation, which decreases with $\sqrt{N}$. A 95% confidence interval for the target value is therefore approximately given by

$$ \left[\mu+\Phi(0.05)^{-1}\sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}N};\mu+\Phi(0.95)^{-1}\sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}N}\right] $$ where $\Phi(\cdot)^{-1}$ is the inverse of the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution.

In[3]:
def BlackScholesMC(S, K, sgm, r, T, callput, n):
    np.random.seed(0)
    w = np.random.standard_normal(n)
    ST = S * np.exp((r - 0.5 * sgm**2) * T + sgm * np.sqrt(T) * w)
    payoff = callput * (ST - K)
    payoff = payoff * (payoff > 0)
    MC = np.exp(-r * T) * np.mean(payoff)
    MCstd = np.exp(-r * T) * np.std(payoff) / np.sqrt(n)
    return MC, MCstd

Dynamic hedging of a Black/Scholes call option

For option sellers, managing the risk associated with option positions is crucial. One common approach to mitigate this risk is through dynamic hedging, which involves continuously adjusting the hedge position in the underlying asset to offset changes in the option's value. In this section, we will implement a dynamic hedging strategy for a European call option using the Black-Scholes model.

In order to perform dynamic hedging, we need to investigate the option's sensitivity to changes in the underlying asset price. We apply Ito's lemma to the Black-Scholes formula for a European call option: $$ \begin{aligned} dC &= \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} dt + \frac{\partial C}{\partial S} dS + \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial S^2} (dS)^2 \\ \end{aligned} $$

The simplest dynamic hedging strategy is delta hedging, which involves holding a position in the underlying asset equal to the option's delta ($\Delta$), which is the first derivative of the option price with respect to the underlying asset price: $$ \Delta = \frac{\partial C}{\partial S} = \Phi(d_1) $$ Thus, at each time step, we adjust our position in the underlying asset to match the option's delta. This way, small changes in the underlying asset price will be offset by changes in the value of the option position, effectively neutralizing the risk.

For the rest of the hedging strategy, we will invest into or borrow from a risk-free asset (bond) to finance the purchase of the underlying asset.

Steps to implement the dynamic hedging strategy:

  1. After selling the option, initialize the hedge position by calculating the initial delta and purchasing the corresponding amount of the underlying asset. The rest of the money is invested in the risk-free asset till the maturity.
  2. At a new time step, recalculate the option's delta based on the updated underlying asset price and time to maturity.
  3. Adjust the hedge position by buying or selling the underlying asset to match the new delta. Update the position in the risk-free asset accordingly.
  4. Any profits from rebalancing the hedge are reinvested in the risk-free asset and any losses are financed by borrowing from the risk-free asset.
  5. Repeat steps 2-4 until the option reaches maturity.

The profits/losses from daily rebalancing are given by:

$$ \Pi(t_i) = S(t_i) \left( d_1(t_i)-d_1(t_{i-1})\right) - K e^{-r(T-t_i)} \left( \Phi(d_2(t_i)) - \Phi(d_2(t_{i-1}))\right) $$

where $d_1(t_i)$ and $d_2(t_i)$ are calculated at time $t_i$ and $t_{i-1}$ respectively.

In[8]:
def BlackScholesHedgePayoff(S, K, sgm, r, T, callput):
    time_to_mat = np.tile(np.linspace(T, 0, S.shape[1]), (S.shape[0], 1))
    d1 = (np.log(S[:, :-1] / K) + (r + (sgm**2) / 2) * time_to_mat[:, :-1]) / (
        sgm * np.sqrt(time_to_mat[:, :-1])
    )
    d2 = d1 - (sgm * np.sqrt(time_to_mat[:, :-1]))
    payoff = np.sum(
        S[:, 1:-1]
        * np.exp(r * time_to_mat[:, 1:-1])
        * (norm.cdf(callput * d1[:, :-1]) - norm.cdf(callput * d1[:, 1:]))
        - K * (norm.cdf(callput * d2[:, :-1]) - norm.cdf(callput * d2[:, 1:])),
        axis=1,
    )
    payoff += S[:, -1] * norm.cdf(callput * d1[:, -1]) - K * norm.cdf(
        callput * d2[:, -1]
    )
    payoff = callput * payoff - np.maximum(0, callput * (S[:, -1] - K))
    return payoff

Monte Carlo simulation of stock price paths and dynamic hedging

To evaluate the effectiveness of the dynamic hedging strategy, we can simulate multiple stock price paths using Monte Carlo methods and apply the hedging strategy to each path. The simulation can be performed as follows: $$S(t_i)=S(t_{i-1})\exp\left\{\left(r\frac12\sigma^\top\sigma\right)(t_i - t_{i-1})+\sigma^\top (W^Q(t_i)-W^Q(t_{i-1}))\right\}$$ where $W^Q(t_i)-W^Q(t_{i-1}) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, (t_i - t_{i-1}) I_d)$, t_i are the discrete time steps.

In[9]:
def simulate_gbm_paths(S0, r, sgm, T, steps, n):
    np.random.seed(0)
    dt = T / steps
    S = np.zeros((n, steps + 1))
    S[:, 0] = S0
    for t in range(1, steps + 1):
        z = np.random.standard_normal(n)
        S[:, t] = S[:, t - 1] * np.exp((r - 0.5 * sgm**2) * dt + sgm * np.sqrt(dt) * z)
    return S

drift = np.linspace(-0.05, 0.1, 51)


def process_step(x):
    S = simulate_gbm_paths(100, 0.05, 0.2, 1, 1000, 1000)
    payoffs = BlackScholesHedgePayoff(S, 100, 0.2, x, 1, 1)
    MC = np.mean(payoffs)
    MCstd = np.std(payoffs)
    return MC, MCstd


results = Parallel(n_jobs=-1)(delayed(process_step)(i) for i in drift)
mean, std = zip(*results)
fig, axs = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 5))
axs[0].plot(
    drift,

    linestyle="-",

axs[0].axvline(x=0.05, color="red", linestyle="--", label="True Drift")
axs[0].set_title("Mean P/L vs drift")
axs[0].set_xlabel("Drift")
axs[0].set_ylabel("Value")
axs[0].grid(True)
axs[1].plot(
    drift,
    std,
    linestyle="-",
    color="black",
)
axs[1].axvline(x=0.05, color="red", linestyle="--", label="True Drift")
axs[1].set_title("Standard Deviation P/L vs drift")
axs[1].set_xlabel("Drift")
axs[1].set_ylabel("Value")
axs[1].grid(True)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

From the results, we can see that when the estimated drift (the drift that is used to perform the hedging) is far away from the true drift (the drift that is used to simulate the stock price paths), the standard deviation of the P/L tends to decrease to 0 and the mean tends to decrease to a large negative value. This indicates that the hedging strategy becomes less effective as the estimated drift deviates from the true drift, leading to larger losses. This can be explained as when the estimated drift is far away from the true drift, the strategy will be over-hedging or under-hedging the option position, resulting in significant losses over time. The hedging strategy relies on accurate estimates of the underlying asset's drift to effectively neutralize risk, and deviations from the true drift can lead to suboptimal hedging decisions.
